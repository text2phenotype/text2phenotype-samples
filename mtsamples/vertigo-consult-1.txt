				<p>The patient is having recurrent attacks of imbalance rather than true vertigo following the history of head trauma and loss of consciousness.  Symptoms are not accompanied by tinnitus or deafness. </p>
<p>Patient had a normal MRI and normal neurological examination on August 24, 2010.</p>
<p>Assessment for peripheral vestibular function follows:<br />Most clinical tests were completed with difficulty and poor cooperation.<br /><b>OTOSCOPY:</b> showed bilateral intact tympanic membranes with central  Weber test and bilateral positive Rinne.<br /><b>ROMBERG TEST:</b> maintained postural stability with difficulty.<br />Frenzel glasses examination: no spontaneous, end gaze nystagmus.<br /><b>DIX-HALLPIKE:</b> showed no positional nystagmus excluding benign paroxysmal positional vertigo.<br /><b>HEAD SHAKING AND VESTIBULOCULAR REFLEX [HALMAGYI TEST]:</b> were done with difficulty a short corrective saccades may give the possibility if having a decompensated vestibular hypofunction. </p>
<p><b>IMPRESSION:</b> Decompensation vestibular hypofunction documented by further electronystagmography and caloric testing. </p>
<p><b>PLAN:</b>  Booked for electronystagmography and advised to continue with her vestibular rehabilitation exercises, in addition to supportive medical treatment in the form of betahistine 24 mg twice a day.</p>
